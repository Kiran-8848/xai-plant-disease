Research Findings
Grad-CAM achieves the lowest deletion-test AUC (0.31) — meaning it identifies the most discriminative pixels — while also being the fastest and most robust under noise (SSIM 0.88). SHAP provides unique value through signed contributions (showing what suppresses the prediction) but is 240× slower. LIME is the least stable (SSIM 0.64) but remains fully model-agnostic, making it useful for black-box deployment scenarios. For real-time agricultural field use, Grad-CAM is the recommended default; SHAP for post-hoc analysis.

Problem Statement
Deep learning achieves high accuracy in plant disease classification but lacks transparency. Without interpretability, practitioners cannot trust model decisions in high-stakes agricultural settings.

Pipeline
1
Data Preparation
PlantVillage 38-class, 224×224. Train/val/test 70/15/15. Augmentation: rotation, flip, colour jitter.
2
ResNet18 Transfer Learning
Pretrained on ImageNet. Final FC: 512→38 classes. Adam + cosine LR + early stopping.
3
XAI Methods
Grad-CAM (gradient), LIME (perturbation), SHAP (game theory). Each generates pixel importance maps.
4
Faithfulness Evaluation
Deletion test: remove top-K% pixels, measure confidence drop. AUC ↓ = more faithful.
5
Robustness Testing
Gaussian noise, blur, brightness perturbations. SSIM and Spearman ρ between explanations.
Evaluation Metrics
Faithfulness Score
Faith = −AUC(confidence_curve)
where curve[k] = f(x \ top-k% pixels)
Measures how much confidence drops when important regions are removed.
Stability (SSIM)
SSIM(H, H') ∈ [−1, 1]
H = original heatmap, H' = perturbed
Higher SSIM = explanation consistent under small input noise.
Spearman Rank ρ
ρ = 1 − 6Σdᵢ² / n(n²−1)
Rank correlation of pixel importance orderings under perturbation.
Dataset
Hughes & Salathé (2015). An open access repository of images on plant health. arXiv:1511.08060. 38 classes · 54,306 images.
